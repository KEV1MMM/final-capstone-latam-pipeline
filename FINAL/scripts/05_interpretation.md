# Key Findings: Financial Wellbeing of Young Professionals in Latin America
**Encuesta de Bienestar Financiero LatAm 2025 — Futuro Digital LatAm**
*Prepared for programme leadership. Based on survey responses from 500 young professionals (ages 18–32) across six countries.*

---

## Finding 1: Income Levels Vary Sharply Across the Region — Programmes Cannot Use a One-Size-Fits-All Approach

Monthly incomes range from a median of USD 798 in Argentina to USD 1,458 in Brazil — a gap of nearly 83% between the lowest- and highest-earning countries in the sample. Chile (USD 1,246) and México (USD 1,067) form a middle tier, while Colombia (USD 857) and Perú (USD 822) sit close to Argentina at the lower end. This is not simply a matter of degree: the absolute amounts that participants can realistically save, invest, or allocate to debt repayment differ so substantially that a savings target or budgeting rule calibrated for Brazil would be financially out of reach for most Argentine respondents. **Recommendation:** Develop country-specific budget templates and savings benchmarks rather than regional averages; the programme content for Argentina, Colombia, and Perú should prioritise low-income strategies (micro-savings, expense reduction, debt management), while materials for Brazil and Chile can more ambitiously address investment and wealth-building.

---

## Finding 2: Savings Grow Sharply With Age — But the Youngest Cohort Is Being Left Behind

Participants aged 18–22 save an average of just USD 61 per month, representing a savings rate of 5.7% of income. By ages 29–32, this rises to USD 154 per month — a savings rate of 15.5%. While the upward trend is encouraging, it also reveals that the youngest participants are accumulating very little financial buffer during their most formative years: someone who saves USD 61/month at 18 is building an emergency fund at a rate that would take over two years to reach one month's average income. The 26–28 age group shows the steepest relative jump (from 8.3% to 11.7%), suggesting a financial turning point that the programme could deliberately accelerate. **Recommendation:** Design a dedicated entry-level module for participants under 23 that focuses on the habit of saving — not the amount — using automatic micro-transfers, a 1% savings challenge, or a "first emergency fund" goal, with the explicit aim of shortening the runway to meaningful financial security.

---

## Finding 3: Housing and Food Consume More Than Half of Every Pay Cheque — Leaving Little Room for Error

On average, participants spend 28.5% of their monthly income on housing and 23.8% on food — a combined 52.3% of earnings consumed before any other obligation is considered. Transport (10.0%), entertainment (8.7%), education (8.5%), and healthcare (4.9%) account for most of the remainder, leaving a thin margin for savings and an even thinner one for unexpected expenses. Critically, healthcare spending at just 4.9% of income is the lowest tracked category — a likely sign of underinvestment rather than good health, particularly for a cohort earning under USD 1,000/month who may be forgoing preventive care due to cost. **Recommendation:** Anchor programme budgeting modules around the reality of this spending structure; introduce the 50/30/20 rule (needs/wants/savings) as an aspirational target and help participants identify specific, practical reductions in their top two categories — for example, analysing housing alternatives or reducing food waste — rather than asking them to cut entertainment or education, which are already modest.

---

## Finding 4: Credit Card Holders Spend More but Also Save More — The Card Itself Is Not the Problem

Despite similar incomes (card holders earn just 1.5% more than non-holders), credit card users spend 16.1% more on food and 17.2% more on entertainment each month, yet still end up saving 6.7% more on average (USD 102 vs. USD 95). This suggests that card holders are not simply overspending relative to their means — they appear to have slightly better overall financial control, possibly because they are already more financially engaged. The risk, however, is that higher discretionary spending supported by a credit line can quickly become problematic when income fluctuates, particularly for the 46.8% of the full sample who already carry debt. **Recommendation:** Rather than treating credit cards as inherently harmful, the programme should teach responsible card mechanics — statement cycles, interest calculation, and the cost of minimum payments — so that the spending confidence card holders already demonstrate is matched by an understanding of the credit trap; pair this with a module specifically for the segment that holds a card *and* carries debt, which likely represents the group most at risk.

---

## Finding 5: AI Tool Usage Is Strongly Linked to Financial Satisfaction — But Income Is the Hidden Variable

Participants who use AI tools heavily (11+ hours/week) report an average financial satisfaction score of 3.43 out of 5, compared to 2.05 for low users — a difference of 1.38 points on a 5-point scale. The Pearson correlation between weekly AI tool hours and satisfaction is r = 0.57 (p < 0.0001), which is a moderately strong relationship. However, high AI users also earn more than twice as much on average (USD 1,750 vs. USD 747), which means income is a significant confounding factor: it is impossible to conclude from this data alone that using AI tools *causes* greater financial satisfaction. What the data does confirm is that digitally engaged participants — who are already higher earners — are using these tools as part of a broader financially active lifestyle. **Recommendation:** Integrate practical AI and digital financial tool literacy into the programme curriculum (budgeting apps, automated savings platforms, AI-based expense trackers), targeting lower-income participants first, as this is where the digital engagement gap is largest and where closing it may yield the greatest relative improvement in financial confidence.

---

## Finding 6: Housing Costs Are a Structural Crisis in Argentina and Chile — Not a Budgeting Problem

Argentina and Chile stand out as the two countries where housing exceeds the internationally recognised 30% affordability threshold: Argentine participants spend an average of 34.1% of income on housing, Chilean participants 32.6%. This is especially striking for Argentina, where it is the country with the *lowest* median income in the sample (USD 798/month), meaning residents are paying the highest relative share of the smallest pay cheques. México sits just below the threshold at 28.1%, while Brazil, Colombia, and Perú remain more affordable at 25–27%. For participants in Argentina and Chile, housing costs are not a behaviour to be corrected through financial education — they are a structural constraint that limits what any other financial strategy can achieve. **Recommendation:** For Argentina- and Chile-specific programme cohorts, reframe the housing conversation away from "reduce your housing costs" (often not feasible) and toward complementary strategies: income diversification, shared housing financial planning, rental negotiation skills, and understanding housing subsidy or social programme eligibility — equipping participants to work within a constrained reality rather than setting goals that the market makes unattainable.

---

*All figures based on n = 500 survey respondents. Income and spending values are in USD. Country-level analyses reflect the following sample sizes: México n=150, Colombia n=80, Argentina n=70, Chile n=70, Perú n=65, Brasil n=65.*
